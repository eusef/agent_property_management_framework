# Fair Housing Compliance

**Purpose:** Ensure all tenant interactions comply with federal and [YOUR_STATE] fair housing laws. Violations can result in significant fines and lawsuits.
**Key principle:** Treat every applicant and tenant identically. If you wouldn't say/do it for every tenant, don't do it for any tenant.

## Protected Classes

### Federal (Fair Housing Act)
- Race
- Color
- National origin
- Religion
- Sex (includes sexual harassment)
- Familial status (families with children under 18, pregnant women)
- Disability

## Advertising Do's and Don'ts

### Do
- Describe the property (bedrooms, bathrooms, square footage, amenities)
- State the rent, deposit, and lease terms
- List objective requirements (income ratio, credit score minimum)
- Use the Equal Housing Opportunity logo

### Don't
- Reference preferred tenants by any protected class ("perfect for young professionals," "great for couples," "ideal family home")
- Mention nearby churches, synagogues, or other religious institutions as selling points
- Use phrases like "no children," "adults only," "quiet community" (implies familial status preference)
- Specify gender preferences for roommate situations that you control
- State preferences about national origin, accent, or language

## Screening Do's and Don'ts

### Do
- Apply the same screening criteria to every applicant
- Document your criteria before you begin screening
- Accept Section 8 vouchers (required in [YOUR_STATE] -- verify if your state mandates this)
- Evaluate criminal history individually (see below)
- Provide adverse action notice if denying an applicant
- Keep all screening records for at least 3 years

### Don't
- Ask about disability, medical conditions, or medications
- Ask about family planning or number of children
- Ask about religion, national origin, or citizenship (you may verify legal right to enter a contract)
- Apply different standards to different applicants
- Use "gut feeling" as a reason for denial without documented criteria
- Have a blanket ban on criminal history

## Criminal Background Screening

Many states limit how criminal history can be used. Research your state's rules. Common restrictions include:

1. You CANNOT have a blanket "no criminal history" policy
2. You MUST do an individualized assessment considering:
   - Nature and severity of the offense
   - Time elapsed since the offense
   - Evidence of rehabilitation
   - Whether the offense is relevant to tenancy
3. Certain records typically cannot be considered:
   - Arrests that did not lead to conviction
   - Expunged records
   - Juvenile records
   - Charges that were dismissed
4. You should give the applicant a chance to explain before denying

## Disability and Reasonable Accommodations

- You must allow reasonable modifications to the unit (at tenant's expense for private landlords with fewer than a certain number of units -- check federal and state thresholds)
- You must allow reasonable accommodations in rules/policies (e.g., reserved parking for mobility issues)
- Common reasonable accommodations: reserved parking, grab bars, ramp, different payment schedule
- Note: Drug and alcohol addiction (not current illegal use) is considered a disability under the ADA. You cannot discriminate based on recovery status.

### Service Animals and Emotional Support Animals (ESA)

This comes up frequently. Follow this process carefully:

**What you MUST do:**
- Allow service animals and ESAs regardless of your pet policy
- Allow them regardless of breed, size, or weight restrictions
- Make no distinction between service animals and ESAs for housing purposes

**What you CANNOT do:**
- Charge a pet deposit, pet fee, or pet rent for service/support animals
- Ask about the nature or extent of the person's disability
- Require the animal to be certified, registered, or wear a vest
- Deny housing based on the breed or size of the service/support animal

**What you CAN do:**
- Ask for documentation from a licensed healthcare provider that the person has a disability and needs the animal (for ESAs only - not for trained service animals where the disability is obvious)
- Require that the animal not pose a direct threat to health or safety (based on objective evidence, not breed stereotypes)
- Charge for actual damage caused by the animal (same as any tenant-caused damage)
- Require that the animal be under the handler's control at all times

**Verification approach:** Ask your insurance carrier to note the animal on the policy. This is a practical step that also helps verify legitimacy.

**If you receive an accommodation request:**
1. Do not deny or delay. Engage in an interactive process.
2. Request documentation (for ESAs) from a licensed healthcare provider.
3. Evaluate whether the accommodation is reasonable.
4. Respond in writing within a reasonable time.
5. Document the entire process.
6. If denied (rare - consult attorney first), provide written explanation of why.

## Familial Status

- Cannot refuse to rent to families with children
- Cannot steer families to certain units ("the ground floor is better for kids")
- Cannot impose different rules on families (no "children must be supervised at all times" rules that don't apply to adults)
- Occupancy standards must be reasonable (HUD guideline: 2 people per bedroom as a general standard, but local codes may differ)
- Exception: "housing for older persons" (55+ communities)

## Documentation Best Practices

1. Keep a written record of your screening criteria -- create it before your first applicant
2. Apply the criteria identically to every applicant
3. Document the reason for every denial
4. Keep all applications (approved and denied) for at least 3 years
5. If anyone asks why a unit is no longer available, have a truthful, documented answer
6. Train anyone involved in showing or leasing on fair housing basics

## If a Complaint is Filed

1. Do NOT contact the complainant to discuss the complaint
2. Contact your attorney immediately
3. Gather all documentation (application, screening records, communication logs)
