# Asset Protection Guide

**Purpose:** Overview of entity structures and liability protection strategies for small-portfolio landlords (1-10 units). This is educational guidance, not legal advice. Consult an attorney for your specific situation.

---

## The Core Question: Do I Need an LLC?

For landlords with 1-4 units, the consensus among property management professionals is that an LLC is **often overkill**. Here's why:

**Arguments for an LLC:**
- Limits personal liability (in theory, only the LLC's assets are at risk)
- Professional appearance
- Separate tax entity (can choose taxation method)

**Arguments against (for small portfolios):**
- Courts frequently "pierce the corporate veil" if the LLC isn't maintained properly (separate accounts, proper records, no commingling of funds)
- Banks often won't lend to LLCs, or require personal guarantees anyway
- Annual filing fees, registered agent fees, and administrative burden
- Many states have franchise taxes or annual fees for LLCs
- Insurance provides the same or better protection in most scenarios

---

## Better Protection for Small Portfolios

Instead of (or in addition to) an LLC, focus on these protections:

### 1. Insurance (Your Primary Defense)

| Coverage | Minimum Recommended |
|----------|-------------------|
| Liability per property | $500K-$1M per occurrence |
| Umbrella policy | $1M+ (costs only a few hundred dollars/year) |
| Policy type | Landlord policy (NOT homeowner's) |
| Deductible strategy | Higher deductible ($2,500-$5,000) = lower premiums |

An umbrella policy is the single most cost-effective protection for a small landlord. It adds $1M+ in coverage on top of your property policies for roughly $200-400/year.

See `compliance/insurance-tracker.md` for tracking.

### 2. Thorough Tenant Screening

Bad tenants are your #1 liability risk. The best asset protection is never renting to someone who will cause problems. See `templates/checklists/tenant-screening.md`.

### 3. A Solid Lease

Your lease is your primary legal document. It should be:
- Drafted or reviewed by a local landlord-tenant attorney
- Updated regularly to reflect current law
- Comprehensive (covering every foreseeable situation)

### 4. Documentation

Document everything. In a lawsuit, the party with better records usually wins.
- Move-in and move-out inspections with photos
- All communications in writing
- Maintenance records
- Financial records

### 5. Professional Conduct

Courts impose harsher judgments on landlords who behave unprofessionally. Consistent, fair, documented treatment of all tenants is both good business and good legal protection.

## Entity Structure Options (If You Choose to Form One)

| Structure | Best For | Key Features |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Sole Proprietorship | 1-2 units, starting out | No formation required. All income/liability flows to you personally. Simplest option. |
| Single-Member LLC | 1-4 units | Some liability protection. "Disregarded entity" for taxes (still reports on Schedule E). State filing fees apply. |
| Series LLC | Multiple properties | Available in some states. Each property is a separate "series" within one LLC. Limits cross-property liability. |
| Multiple LLCs | Larger portfolios | One LLC per property or group of properties. Maximum isolation but highest administrative burden. |

**If you do form an LLC:**
- Keep LLC finances completely separate from personal finances
- Maintain proper records (meeting minutes, resolutions)
- Don't commingle funds
- Pay the annual fees and maintain good standing
- Get the lease, insurance, and all contracts in the LLC's name

## What to Do Right Now

1. **Get proper insurance** - umbrella policy if you don't have one
2. **Screen tenants thoroughly** - every time, no exceptions
3. **Use a solid lease** - have it reviewed by a local attorney
4. **Document everything** - this framework handles most of it
5. **Consult an attorney** - for entity structure advice specific to your state and situation
