# Lease Term Strategy Guide

**Purpose:** Help you choose the right lease structure for your properties. There is no single correct answer; the best approach depends on your market, tenant quality, and risk tolerance.

---

## Lease Term Options

### Month-to-Month

**How it works:** Tenancy continues indefinitely with either party able to terminate with the state-required notice period (typically 30 days, but varies - check `compliance/landlord-tenant-law.md`).

**Advantages:**
- Maximum flexibility to remove problem tenants
- Can adjust rent with proper notice at any time
- No commitment if you plan to sell

**Disadvantages:**
- Tenant can leave at any time with short notice
- Higher turnover risk and vacancy costs
- Less predictable income

**Best for:** Properties where you want maximum control, or when you're uncertain about a new tenant.

---

### Fixed-Term (12 Months)

**How it works:** Lease locks in both parties for 12 months. At expiration, it either renews or converts to month-to-month (depending on your lease language and state law).

**Advantages:**
- Guaranteed occupancy for one year (tenant can't leave without consequence)
- Predictable income
- Standard in most markets
- Easier to find tenants (most expect 12-month terms)

**Disadvantages:**
- Harder to remove a problem tenant before the term expires
- Must wait for expiration to make major changes

**Best for:** Most residential rentals. This is the default recommendation for small portfolios.

---

### Long-Term Fixed (24-36 Months)

**How it works:** Extended lease with built-in annual rent increases. Some landlords offer a renewal incentive (e.g., one month free) to encourage signing.

**Advantages:**
- Dramatically reduces turnover and vacancy costs
- Built-in annual rent increases compound over the term
- Spring expiration strategy: set all leases to expire March 31 for maximum demand
- Attracts tenants who want stability (often higher quality)

**Disadvantages:**
- Locked in if the market rises faster than your built-in increases
- Harder to remove a problem tenant
- Less common; some tenants won't sign long terms

**Best for:** Stable properties with proven tenants. Works well in markets with consistent demand.

## Rent Increase Strategies

### Built-In Annual Increases

Include an automatic annual increase in the lease (e.g., 2-3% per year). The tenant agrees to the increase schedule at signing, eliminating the need for annual negotiation.

**Example for a 36-month lease at $1,800/month with 3% annual increase:**

| Year | Monthly Rent | Annual Total |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1 | $1,800 | $21,600 |
| 2 | $1,854 | $22,248 |
| 3 | $1,910 | $22,920 |

### Market-Based Increases at Renewal

Research comparable rents before each renewal and adjust accordingly. This captures market changes but requires more effort and can lead to difficult conversations.

**Process:**
1. 120+ days before lease expiration: research market comps
2. 90 days before: send renewal offer with proposed terms (see `templates/comms/lease-renewal.md`)
3. Follow state-required notice periods for rent increases (check `compliance/landlord-tenant-law.md`)

### The Rule: Never Skip a Year

Regardless of strategy, increase rent every year. Even a small 2-3% increase:
- Keeps pace with inflation
- Maintains market positioning
- Prevents the shock of a large catch-up increase later
- The annual-review checklist (`templates/checklists/annual-review.md`) includes a rent analysis section for this purpose

## Lease Expiration Timing

**When should leases expire?**

Spring (March-April) is the strongest rental market in most areas. Demand is highest, giving you the best negotiating position and fastest re-rental if the tenant leaves.

Winter (November-December) is the weakest. Fewer people move during holidays and bad weather, meaning longer vacancies and potentially lower rents.

**Strategy:** If possible, set all new leases to expire in spring. For a tenant moving in mid-year, use a shorter initial term (e.g., 8-10 months) to align the expiration with spring, then convert to standard 12-month terms going forward.

## Lease Structure Decision Matrix

| Situation | Recommended Term | Rent Strategy |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| New tenant, unproven | 12-month fixed | Market-based at renewal |
| Proven tenant, stable | 24-36 month fixed | Built-in 2-3% annual |
| Uncertain market | Month-to-month | Adjust as needed |
| Planning to sell within 2 years | Month-to-month or 12-month | Market-based |
| Premium property, high demand | 12-month fixed | Market-based (you have leverage) |
| Lower-demand area | 24-36 month (lock in good tenants) | Built-in increases |
