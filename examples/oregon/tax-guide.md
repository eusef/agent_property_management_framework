# Oregon Tax Compliance Guide

**Purpose:** Track all tax obligations for rental property income -- federal, Oregon state, Portland local, and contractor reporting.
**Disclaimer:** This is an operational reference, not tax advice. Work with a CPA for filing.

---

## Federal Income Tax (Schedule E)

Rental income and expenses are reported on IRS Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss), filed with your personal 1040.

### What's Reported as Income
- All rent received (gross, before expenses)
- Late fees collected
- Non-refundable deposits or fees
- Any other income from the property

### Deductible Expenses (Common Categories)
- Mortgage interest (from lender 1098)
- Property taxes
- Insurance premiums
- Repairs and maintenance
- Utilities paid by owner
- Property management fees
- Advertising for vacancies
- Travel to/from property for management purposes
- Professional services (lawyer, CPA, etc.)
- Depreciation (see below)
- Home office deduction (if applicable -- portion of home used for PM business)

### Depreciation
- Residential rental property is depreciated over 27.5 years (straight-line)
- Only the building value is depreciable (not land)
- Need: purchase price, land value (from tax assessment), and date placed in service
- Capital improvements are depreciated separately (new roof, HVAC, etc.)

### Passive Activity Rules
- Rental income is generally "passive" for tax purposes
- Losses can only offset other passive income, UNLESS:
  - You are a "real estate professional" (750+ hrs/yr in real estate, majority of working time)
  - Active participation exemption: up to $25,000 in losses can offset ordinary income if AGI < $100,000 (phases out between $100K-$150K)
- Track hours spent on property management to support active participation

## Oregon State Income Tax

Oregon taxes rental income as ordinary income. Filed on Oregon Form 40.

- Oregon generally follows federal treatment for rental income/expenses
- Oregon has its own depreciation rules in some cases (check with CPA)
- Oregon state income tax rates for 2026: 4.75% to 9.9% (confirm current rates)
- Portland residents may also owe Arts Tax ($35/yr flat) and Metro Supportive Housing Tax (1% on taxable income over $125K single / $200K joint)

## Portland Business Tax

Portland-specific taxes for rental income:

| Tax | Rate | Notes |
|-----|------|-------|
| Portland Business License Tax (BLT) | 2.6% of net income | All rental income from Portland properties |
| Multnomah County Business Income Tax (MCBIT) | 2.0% of net income | EXEMPT for landlords with fewer than 10 residential units |
| Rental Registration Fee | $70/unit/year | Filed with Combined Business Tax Return |

**Filing:** Combined Business Tax Return + Schedule R, due April 15 (calendar year filers). File with Portland Revenue Division.

## Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

If you expect to owe $1,000+ in federal tax (or $1,000+ Oregon state) after withholding and credits, you should make quarterly estimated payments to avoid underpayment penalties.

### Federal Estimated Tax (Form 1040-ES)

| Quarter | Period | Due Date |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Q1 | Jan 1 - Mar 31 | April 15 |
| Q2 | Apr 1 - May 31 | June 15 |
| Q3 | Jun 1 - Aug 31 | September 15 |
| Q4 | Sep 1 - Dec 31 | January 15 (following year) |

### Oregon Estimated Tax (Form 40-ESV)

Same quarterly schedule as federal.

## 1099 Reporting

### 1099-NEC (Non-Employee Compensation)
- Required for any contractor/vendor paid $600+ in a calendar year
- Due to recipient: January 31
- Due to IRS: January 31
- File electronically via IRS FIRE system or through tax software

### 1099-MISC
- Used for rent payments to a landlord (if you sublease) -- likely N/A for most owners
- Also used for certain other payments (prizes, legal settlements, etc.)

### Who Does NOT Need a 1099
- Corporations (S-corp or C-corp) -- generally exempt (but lawyers always get one regardless)
- Payments made via credit card or payment apps (Venmo, PayPal, Zelle business) -- the payment processor issues the 1099-K instead

**Important:** Collect W-9 forms from every contractor BEFORE paying them. Much harder to get after the fact.

## Annual Tax Calendar

| Date | Item | Action |
|------|------|--------|
| January 31 | 1099-NEC due | File with IRS and send to all contractors paid $600+ |
| April 15 | Q1 estimated tax due | Federal (1040-ES) and Oregon (40-ESV) |
| April 15 | Prior year tax return due | Federal 1040 + Schedule E, Oregon Form 40 |
| April 15 | Portland Combined Business Tax Return | Portland BLT (2.6%) + Schedule R ($70/unit) |
| June 15 | Q2 estimated tax due | Federal and Oregon |
| September 15 | Q3 estimated tax due | Federal and Oregon |
| October 15 | Extended tax return due | If filed extension |
| January 15 | Q4 estimated tax due | Federal and Oregon |

## Records to Keep

Maintain for at least 3 years (IRS) / 4 years (Oregon) after filing. Keep 7 years to be safe.

- All income records (rent payments, other income)
- All expense receipts and invoices
- Mortgage interest statements (1098)
- Property tax records
- Insurance premium records
- Contractor invoices and W-9s
- Depreciation schedules
- Mileage logs (if claiming travel)
- Home office documentation (if claiming)
- Owner statements from PM company (historical)
- Bank statements for operating and deposit accounts
